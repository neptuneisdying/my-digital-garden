***1. Glycolysis***

Glycolysis is the first stage of respiration. It occurs outside of the mitochondria, in the cytoplasm.

![Pasted image 20260212195349.png](/img/user/Images/Pasted%20image%2020260212195349.png)

1. Glucose is phosphorylated at each end to form glucose phosphate. It has 2 ends, so two ATP used. This forms 2x TP (You'll remember these from [[School/A-Levels/Biology/Paper 2/Topic 5 - Energy Transfers In and Between Organisms/5.1 - Photosynthesis\|5.1 - Photosynthesis]] in the *Light Independent Reaction*).
2. Each triose phosphate is oxidised by NAD, forming NADH.
3. Each triose phosphate phosphorylates 2 ADP to form 4 ATP total. Triose phosphate only has one phosphate group so to do this, it requires another Pi.
4. The end product is 2 Pyruvate, and the overall yield of glycolysis is 2 ATP.

***2. Link Reaction***

![Pasted image 20260212200104.png](/img/user/Images/Pasted%20image%2020260212200104.png)

The Link Reaction is the second step of respiration, and this occurs in the mitochondria (therefore it is exclusive to aerobic respiration).

1. Pyruvate is oxidised by NAD to form NADH, and releases a carbon in the form of CO2, forming the 2 carbon molecule Acetate.
2. Acetate reacts with the coenzyme A to form Acetyl-CoA.

***3. Krebs Cycle***

![Pasted image 20260212200428.png](/img/user/Images/Pasted%20image%2020260212200428.png)

Following after the Link Reaction in the mitochondria is the Krebs cycle.

1. AcetylCoA reacts with a 4C compound (which you don't need to know the name of) releasing the CoA and forming a 6C compound.
2. The 6C carbon releases 2 carbons in the form 2 CO2 molecules.
3. It also reduces 3 NAD to 3 NADH.
4. It also reduces 1 FAD to FADH2.
5. One ADP is phosphorylated to form 1 ATP. Phosphorylation in this manner in substrate-level phosphorylation.
6. The 6C lost 2C, and after everything done it is now that unnamed 4C compound, so it's a cycle. (A *Krebs Cycle*, thanks for watching).

To remember that 3:1 NAD to FAD reduction ratio, just remember the numbers and the fact that FAD is kinda irrelevant.

***4. Other Molecules in Respiration***

Other molecules than glucose can contribute to the continuation of these parts of respiration.

![Pasted image 20260212201234.png](/img/user/Images/Pasted%20image%2020260212201234.png)

- Glycerol from triglycerides can form triose phosphate for use in glycolysis.
- Fatty acids from triglycerides can be used to form Acetyl-CoA for use in the Krebs Cycle.
- 3 amino acids can be used to form pyruvate for the link reaction, whilst 4 or 5 amino acids can be used to form 4C/5C intermediates in the Krebs cycle.

***5. Oxidative Phosphorylation***

![Pasted image 20260212201827.png](/img/user/Images/Pasted%20image%2020260212201827.png)

This part is quite similar to the *Light Dependent Reaction* in [[School/A-Levels/Biology/Paper 2/Topic 5 - Energy Transfers In and Between Organisms/5.1 - Photosynthesis\|5.1 - Photosynthesis]]. If you can remember that, you can remember this.

1. Remember all that NADH we made before (and the FADH2 but nobody really cares about that)? All of those lose a proton and electron, and the FADH2 loses 2 protons and 2 electrons, reforming NAD and FAD.
2. The electrons move down a series of protein complexes called the Electron Transfer Chain (this is the real ETC not the one in the thylakoid membrane) in a series of redox reactions.
3. These reactions provide energy for the active transport of those protons we released earlier from the matrix into the intermembrane space. This space is very small so that there is a low volume, meaning the concentration of protons in that space is super high, increasing the proton gradient.
4. the protons move back down this concentration gradient in a process called chemiosmosis via the ATP synthase. The movement of protons powers the phosphorylation of ADP to form ATP.
5. The ETC remains continuous because oxygen is the final electron acceptor at the end of the ETC, reacting with protons and electrons to form water. This is why it is aerobic respiration, since we need oxygen.

This is the real heavy hitter of aerobic respiration when it comes to producing ATP. Glycolysis has a yield of 2 ATP per glucose, and Krebs also has a yield of 2 ATP per glucose, but oxidative phosphorylation can have a yield of upwards of 30 ATP.

***6. Anaerobic Respiration***

Anaerobic respiration occurs purely in the cytoplasm, and follows after glycolysis.

![Pasted image 20260212210044.png](/img/user/Images/Pasted%20image%2020260212210044.png)

**In Animals:**

After triose phosphate is oxidised to pyruvate, it is reduced again to form lactate. this is to reform NAD so the reduction of TP can continue for substrate-level phosphorylation, 2 ATP at a time.

**In Plants:**

Exact same, except instead of lactate being formed, it's ethanol and CO2.
