***1. Introductory Information***

Photosynthesis is how plants use energy from light to synthesise glucose.

Overall equation: water + carbon dioxide -> glucose + oxygen

There are two stages to photosynthesis, the *Light Dependent Reaction* and the *Light Independent Reaction*.

***2. Chloroplasts***

Chloroplasts are the site of photosynthesis. They are adapted for photosynthesis, and are believed to be bacteria which were absorbed by earlier plant cells: this is called endosymbiosis.

![Pasted image 20260212182423.png](/img/user/Images/Pasted%20image%2020260212182423.png)
- Thylakoid membranes are where the light dependent reaction occurs.
- Stacks of thylakoid membranes form grana. This provides a large surface area for chlorophyll, electrons and enzymes to attach.
- Proteins in the grana hold the chlorophyll in a specific manner to absorb the maximum amount of light.
- The granal/thylakoid membrane has ATP synthase channels for the LDR, allowing ATP to be synthesised and a proton gradient to be established.
- Chloroplasts contain DNA and ribosomes which allow them to synthesise proteins needed in the LDR.

***3. Light Dependent Reaction***

![Pasted image 20260212190502.png](/img/user/Images/Pasted%20image%2020260212190502.png)

1. Light hits the chlorophyll molecules in Photosystem II (PSII), causing the electrons to become excited. This is photoionisation.
2. Photoionisation drives the process of photolysis, which is when water is split to release 2 protons, 2 electrons and oxygen. **(Key Note: In the LDR, oxygen is NOT the final electron acceptor, like it is in [[School/A-Levels/Biology/Paper 2/Topic 5 - Energy Transfers In and Between Organisms/5.2 - Respiration\|5.2 - Respiration]]. Remember that oxygen is the waste product of photosynthesis: it isn't useful.)**
3. The excited electrons leave the PSII and move down a series of protein complexes (the electron transfer chain/ETC) via a series of redox reactions which release energy.
4. The electrons released by photolysis replace the excited electrons in PSII.
5. The energy provided by the excited electrons powers the active transport of protons from the stroma (essentially the matrix of the chloroplast) into the thylakoid space, creating a chemiosmotic gradient.
6. The protons move down this chemiosmotic gradient back into the stroma via ATP synthase, powering the phosphorylation of ADP to form ATP. This process is called photophosphorylation.
7. The process is able to continue because of the continous supply of electrons from water to be excited, and the flow is kept continous because NADP reacts with a proton and an electron to form NADPH, or reduced NADP. This is then important in the *Light Independent Reaction*.

***4. Light Independent Reaction***

![Pasted image 20260212194239.png](/img/user/Images/Pasted%20image%2020260212194239.png)

- CO2 reacts with Ribulose Biphosphate (RuBP) in a reaction catalysed by Rubisco to form 2 Glycerate 3-phosphate (2x GP).
- The GP is then reduced by NADPH from the *LDR* to form 2x triose phosphate (2x TP). This reaction uses ATP.
- Triose Phosphate is what can be used to form organic molecules like glucose, amino acids and glycerol.
- Triose phosphate is used to reform the 5 carbon RuBP, continuing the *LIR* cycle.

This all occurs in the stroma.
