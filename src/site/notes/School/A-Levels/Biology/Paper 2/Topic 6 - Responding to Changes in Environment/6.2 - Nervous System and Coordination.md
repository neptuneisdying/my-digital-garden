***1. Neurone Structure***

Neurones are specialised animal cells that pass on nerve impulses. Motor neurones are what we focus on in Paper 2 Bio, so when they ask about neurone structure, describe this. Some motor neurones are myelinated, some aren't.

![Pasted image 20260212211343.png](/img/user/Images/Pasted%20image%2020260212211343.png)

- Dendrites are the ends of neurones that form synapses with axon terminals.
- Within the dendrites are the cell body, where the nucleus and other organelles are.
- The axon is the long stretch of the neurone where the nerve impulse travels along.
- This is wrapped around by Schwann cells, which forms myelin sheaths.
- The exposed parts between myelin sheaths are called Nodes of Ranvier.

The axon being myelinated means that the action potential (the wave of depolarisation that makes the nerve impulse) is able to travel via *saltatory conduction*, meaning instead of travelling across in a line, the action potential jumps between Nodes of Ranvier.
**(If this isn't making sense, come back to this point after covering action potentials in the neurone.)**

***2. Resting Potential**

![Pasted image 20260212212501.png](/img/user/Images/Pasted%20image%2020260212212501.png)

1. At resting potential, there is a higher conc. of Na+ outside and K+ inside and the outside of the axon is more positive than the inside.
2. The electrochemical gradients for the ions cause them to move along the sodium and potassium ion channels.
3. The sodium-potassium pump maintains the resting potential by pumping Na back out and K back in.

***3. Nerve Impulse***

![Pasted image 20260212214551.png](/img/user/Images/Pasted%20image%2020260212214551.png)

1. When a stimulus occurs, there is an influx of Na+ ions outside of the axon.
2. This causes voltage-gated Na+ ion channels to change shape and open, allowing Na+ ions to move into the axon, making it more positive.
3. If the inside of axon is positively charged enough (relative to the outside of the axon), the threshold potential will be met and an action potential will be triggered and depolarisation will occur.
4. An action potential means that the axon reaches its maximum positive charge due to much more Na+ ions entering the axon.
5. Voltage gated Na+ ion channels then close when the action potential is reached, and voltage gated K+ ion channels open.
6. K+ ions leave the axon, repolarising it and lowering the potential difference.
7. It does this a bit too much, and actually goes below what it was at at resting potential. This is called hyperpolarisation.
8. To increase it back up, Na+ ions diffuse into the axon via the regular Na+ ion channels. whilst the sodium potassium pump re-establishes the electrochemical gradients in the beginning (they are currently reversed).

***4. The All-or-Nothing Principle***

![Pasted image 20260212215246.png](/img/user/Images/Pasted%20image%2020260212215246.png)

The all-or-nothing principle is as it sounds:

*The strength of a nerve impulse has no bearing on the strength of the action potential.*

As long as the threshold is met, there is an action potential.
If it isn't met, no action potential.

Think of it like a lightswitch: It is on or off, no inbetween where the light is half as strong.

When stimulus strength increases, what happens instead is that the frequency of action potentials increases.
